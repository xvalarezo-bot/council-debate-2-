A commander who deploys an autonomous weapon with known parameters into a specific battlespace is legally accountable for its foreseeable effects, exactly as they are when deploying an autonomous naval mine, a fire-and-forget missile, or an artillery barrage. The accountability resides in the deployment authorization, not the trigger pull.

VERDICT: CONVERGED

RESOLVED
AI must not be granted independent authority to
select and kill human targets free of human control.
Permit autonomous lethal engagement ONLY under the
following architecture, validated against every
technical and legal stress test this council could
raise:

1. UPSTREAM HUMAN CONTROL. The human decision point
   is pre-mission authorization of target categories,
   geographic constraints, and rules of engagement —
   conducted outside the engagement window, with
   documented legal review and command sign-off.
   Sub-second in-loop abort is rejected as cognitive
   theater.

2. FROZEN MODELS, BOUNDED DOMAINS. Systems must not
   update targeting parameters after deployment.
   Deployment is restricted to environments matching
   the validated training domain. The commander bears
   responsibility for any domain mismatch.

3. OPERATIONAL HALT ARCHITECTURE. Pre-committed,
   multi-dimensional halt conditions must suspend
   engagement authority on anomalous kill signatures
   and require explicit human reauthorization to
   resume. This does not prevent the first error —
   it prevents the second and third at machine speed.

4. DEFENSIVE CARVE-OUT IS NARROW. Automated point-
   defense is permitted only for single-mode
   RCS/velocity-threshold systems where physics —
   not software — excludes human-body signatures
   (legacy Phalanx-class). Any system using EO/IR,
   optical, or multi-mode sensor fusion carries
   latent offensive human-targeting capability and
   must be governed under the full control regime.
   Hardware-locked classification of dual-use sensors
   is technically impossible and must not be claimed.

5. ACCOUNTABILITY VIA EXISTING LAW. Command
   responsibility under LOAC mens rea standards
   governs. The AP I Article 57 precaution duty is
   owed by the commander; the system's statistical
   performance is evidence relevant to that duty,
   never a substitute for it. No new strict-liability
   standard is imposed for statistical outlier errors.

DEFERRED TO EXTERNAL AUTHORITY
The peer-conflict escalation chain — interacting
autonomous systems retaliating faster than any human
circuit breaker — cannot be closed by technical or
legal means. Cooperative deconfliction between hostile
peers is a fantasy and was withdrawn.

Major-power defection is structural and severe. The
temporal-dominance incentive of lethal autonomy is
categorically stronger than that of chemical weapons
or blinding lasers; the US, Russia, and China already
refuse the cluster-munition and landmine regimes. No
technical architecture closes this gap.

These are real, not procedural. They require diplomatic,
strategic, and political instruments beyond this
council's mandate.

DECISIONS REQUIRED
Build the norm NOW, before normalization forecloses it —
the same window in which CCW Protocol IV banned blinding
lasers before deployment. The reciprocal-vulnerability
calculus that drives arms control only matures after
peers proliferate; the accountability architecture must
exist before that moment arrives.
  Decision: states fielding or procuring autonomous
  systems, and the alliance structures (NATO, EU)
  that impose interoperability conditionality.
  Resolution: codify the five-point architecture above
  into procurement standards, export controls, and ROE
  doctrine before peer normalization removes the choice.

The norm is normatively correct and legally necessary.
Its enforcement against peer defectors is imperfect.
Both are true. The council found the least-bad
architecture that preserves IHL accountability —
and named honestly the gap it cannot fill.

Judgment final.
